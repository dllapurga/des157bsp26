Annoted Bibliography

Berge, Jerica M., et al. “Intergenerational Transmission of Dietary Behaviors and the Role of Family Meals.” Appetite, vol. 105, 2016, pp. 191–199.

This study explores how dietary habits and food traditions are passed across multiple generations within families. The authors focus on how family meals influence long-term eating practices, cultural habits, and social connection. Their research demonstrates that consistent exposure to cultural food practices strengthens identity and increases the likelihood that traditions will continue into future generations. This source is valuable to my project because it provides evidence for the importance of preserving culinary practices before they're lost. The authors express that intentional family food traditions create stronger generational continuity. I believe this reinforces the purpose of my project which is to digitally preserve recipes so descendants can maintain a meaningful connection to their family’s cultural heritage.

DeVault, Marjorie L. “Between Mothers and Markets: Constructing Family Identity Through Homemade Food.” Journal of Consumer Culture, vol. 4, no. 3, 2004, pp. 319–340.

DeVault examines how homemade food contributes to family identity and emotional connection. The article argues that preparing food within the home creates a sense of intimacy and continuity that can't be replicated through commercialized alternatives such as restaurants or packaged meals. The author discusses how family cooking practices communicate care, belonging, and cultural authenticity. This source is especially relevant to my project because it supports my argument that restaurant versions of cultural dishes cannot replace the unique preparation methods passed through generations. DeVault expresses that homemade food plays a central role in constructing family identity. I agree with this because my project aims to preserve these authentic family-specific culinary practices in an interactive digital archive.

Grace, Tina. “Foodways and Identity: Narratives of Life History Told Through Family Recipes.” Indiana University Southeast Undergraduate Research Journal, vol. 2, 2019, pp. 1–9, scholarworks.iu.edu/journals/index.php/iusburj/article/view/29390.

This article examines the relationship between family recipes and cultural identity, arguing that recipes serve as narratives that preserve personal and collective memory. Grace explains that food carries emotional, historical, and cultural meaning that extends far beyond fulfilling hunger. The article highlights how recipes act as vessels for storytelling, passing down traditions, values, and memories through generations. This source is highly useful to my project because it supports my idea that recipes are not simply instructions for cooking but are archives of cultural heritage. Grace concludes that preserving recipes helps sustain family histories. I agree with this conclusion because my project seeks to create a digital space where these histories can be preserved and shared for future generations.
